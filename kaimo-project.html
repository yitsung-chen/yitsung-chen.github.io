Overview

Qaimo ( recently rebranded as Kaimo ) is a free mobile and web platform for creating personalized research experiments to explore the impacts of your habits on your health. Our group incorporated with the startup team to conduct an usability research for its beta released product in order to align the design pragmatism with real-world scenarios. Research was conducted based on both qualitative and quantitative methodologies.

As the sole analyst in our group, I was responsible for the end-to-end quantitative analysis, including methodology research, metrics definition, and data visualization to quantify the testing results, which validated and further supported the qualitative findings. The data collected throughout the testing process, where 9 participants were recruited as the testing subjects. Each subject was asked to complete 4 scenario tasks designed to fully test the product functionality.

Methodology

The quantitative metrics applied in our data analysis were served to measure the 3 core foundations of product design: Effectiveness, Efficiency and Satisfaction. While multiple formula options were provided for different analysis purposes, we chose those that optimized the following principles: (1) Measurable impact, (2) Avoided unexpected confusion and (3) Simplified the data collection process.

Qualitative

The contextual interviews were first prototyped based on our initial walk-through on the product interface, and underwent 2 rounds of expert evaluation to be finalized.

  • Scenario #1 : Learn about the Qaimo's home page.
  • Scenario #2 : Login and run a specific experiment then edit the given data.
  • Scenario #3 : End the experiment and writing down the conclusions.
  • Scenario #4 : Initiate your own experiment from scratch.

Upon completed the interviews, Jakob's Heuristic Principles were kicked in to help structure our final solutions

Quantitative

Metrics formulas were based on this resource. Feel free to check out for more details.

  • Effectiveness : Time-based Efficiency
    Benchmark subjects efficiency against skilled user's, expert efficiency, measure unit: (goal / time-unit).
  • Efficiency : Number of Errors
    Benchmark subjects total clicks per task against the minimum clicks for the same task, any additional click would be treated as redundancy error.
  • Satisfaction : SEQ Score
    Score range was 1 to 10, where 1 been the easiest and 10 been the hardest.

Research Findings

Our recommendations below listed out some of the major issues occured along the testing process. Sythesized across heuristics review, qualitative feedbacks ( See the most frequent comments from the subjects below ), and supported by the quantitative analysis ( See visualizations below ).

Most Frequent Feedbacks

" Profile page is too wordy with no clear interpretations, I don't know how my data will be handled and whether the application is free or not? "

" The buttons are not salient to me, not sure what is clickable and what is not."

" No clue on how to End Experiment, there's no clear instruction on how to do so. "

" What's the difference between AB Test and Randomized Trials? Think I'll just randomly pick one to design my experiment."

Quantitative Analysis

1. Efficiency

Scenario 2 & 3 were much suitable to be measured by this metrics due to their straightforward testing purposes in which we designed each task with only 1 solution. The ideal case for a good design is to minimize the gap between skilled users' and novice users' performance.

Scenario 3 resulted in shorter gap compared to Scenario 2, but also had the greatest range and variation among all other tasks. With such observation and a minor differences in fact, the design involved in these 2 scenarios were all worth investigating.

2. Effectiveness

Scenario 3 led to way more errors compared to the rest yet also had the most efficient completion rate. The most frequent frustration encountered during this scenario was inability to find the “end study” function , resulting in many extraneous clicks among the incorrect tabs.

For Scenario 4's, 5 out of all 9 subjects created a scientifically invalid experiments. The 100% completion rate regarded to this task was somewhat due to users' blindly guessing and random chance, which were justified by qualitative user feedbacks.



3. Satisifaction

Though none of the scenarios were rated as absurdly high, all testing results indicated certain levels of frustration from the users while interacted with the product.

Specifically the scenarios involved with saving data, editing and finding the "end study” function were rated as the highest difficulty. As a result, our recommendations relevant to these were of higher priority.
